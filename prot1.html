Course Activities

Activity 1: Essay on Studying Rizal

February 6, 2025 (submitted late)

This activity required writing a 300-word essay on the importance of studying the life, works, and writing of Dr. Jose Rizal. I explored how Rizal's contributions to Philippine literature, social reform, and national identity continue to be relevant for modern Filipino students.

Summary: In my essay, I discussed how studying Rizal provides crucial insights into Philippine history during the Spanish colonial period and the emergence of Filipino nationalism. I emphasized how Rizal's works like "Noli Me Tangere" and "El Filibusterismo" served as powerful social commentaries that awakened national consciousness. Furthermore, I explored how his ideals of education, peaceful reform, and cultural pride offer valuable lessons for contemporary Filipino society. I concluded by reflecting on how Rizal's multifaceted talents as a writer, scientist, artist, and polyglot exemplify the kind of holistic excellence that today's students should aspire to achieve.

Activity 2: Analysis of the Rizal Bill

February 18, 2025

This activity required researching and answering questions about the Rizal Bill, including its sponsorship in Congress, arguments supporting it, and opposition against it.

Summary: In my response, I detailed how Senator Claro M. Recto was the primary sponsor of the Rizal Bill (Republic Act 1425) in Congress. The bill, which mandated the teaching of Rizal's life and works in all schools, was supported with arguments that emphasized the need to instill patriotism, promote national identity, and preserve cultural heritage among Filipino students. Supporters believed that Rizal's writings offered important insights into Philippine history and inspired critical thinking about social issues. Opposition to the bill came primarily from religious sectors, particularly the Catholic Church, which expressed concerns about Rizal's critical portrayal of the clergy in his novels and worried about potential anti-Catholic sentiment. Some critics also questioned the focus on a single national hero at the expense of others. Despite this opposition, the bill was passed in 1956 and remains a cornerstone of Philippine education policy.

Activity 3: Philippine Heroes Analysis

February 18, 2025

This activity examined the concept of "Pangangayaw" and analyzed the criteria for recognizing national heroes in the context of Dr. Jose Rizal and other Filipino revolutionary figures.

Summary: In my analysis, I explored how "Pangangayaw" refers to pre-colonial Filipino maritime raids or expeditions that demonstrated bravery and leadership, establishing historical precedents for recognizing heroism in Philippine culture. The three main criteria for recognizing heroes that were established during the American colonial period included: significant contribution to nation-building, exemplary moral character, and international recognition. Dr. Jose Rizal fulfilled these criteria through his literary works that inspired nationalism, his peaceful advocacy for reform, and his recognition by international scholars and leaders. By contrast, revolutionary figures like Gregorio del Pilar, Andrés Bonifacio, and Emilio Aguinaldo were not officially designated as national heroes based on American criteria because their revolutionary activities were seen as potentially inspiring continued resistance against American rule, their methods involved armed struggle rather than peaceful reform, and they lacked the same level of international recognition as Rizal. This selective heroification revealed how the American colonial government strategically promoted Rizal as a model of peaceful reform rather than revolutionary resistance.

Reflections on Major Reports

Reflection on Report 1: WHO is Rizal

This report deepened my understanding of Jose Rizal as a multifaceted historical figure beyond the one-dimensional portrayal often presented in basic education. I learned that Rizal was not just a writer and national hero, but also a physician, linguist, artist, scientist, and global traveler whose worldview was shaped by his experiences across Europe and Asia.

What surprised me most was discovering the complexity of Rizal's personal life and relationships, including his connections with family members and romantic partners, which humanized him beyond his heroic image. I also gained new perspective on how his educational background in Europe influenced his progressive ideas about social reform, science, and education.

This report challenged me to think more critically about how we construct national heroes and the importance of understanding historical figures within their complete context. As an InfoTech student, it made me reflect on how digital archives and resources can help preserve and provide access to primary sources that reveal more nuanced understandings of historical figures like Rizal.

Reflection on Report 2: Leadership in Rizal

Exploring Rizal's leadership qualities provided me with valuable insights into different models of influence and advocacy. What stood out to me was how Rizal led primarily through intellectual and moral influence rather than direct political or military command, demonstrating how writing, education, and personal example can be powerful tools for social change.

I was particularly struck by Rizal's commitment to education as a vehicle for national development and his belief that enlightening minds was the first step toward meaningful reform. His focus on developing critical thinking skills among his countrymen rather than simply inspiring rebellion showed remarkable foresight and strategic thinking.

This report has influenced my own approach to leadership as a technology student. In collaborative projects, I now appreciate how sharing knowledge, maintaining ethical standards, and communicating ideas clearly can be more effective than simply directing others. Rizal's example has encouraged me to consider how my technical skills could be used to address social challenges and contribute to community development, much as he used his medical training to serve his hometown of Calamba.
